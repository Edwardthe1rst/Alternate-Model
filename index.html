From the Desk of Edwardthe1rst

To whom it may concern,
I have written you this note because you probably know a great deal about science or about fact-finding or investigation in general, and you want to know more about another new thing. This new thing, however, may find you immediately dismissing it and moving on because it may possibly seem too dramatic for your taste. So before you look at it, to dampen the drama a bit and make it look a little less ludicrous, I would like to convince you of two things:

All science is modeling.
From Aristotle to Newton to Einstein, from biology to astronomy, science has always been about explaining different parts of the world and how they work, and even now I will attempt to explain some things about the world. These explanations are called models, and always have had some truth to them. Depreciated models about the origin of mice, the position of the earth in relation to the celestial bodies, and the cause of fire are well known because of the debate they sparked long ago. Almost every model has had or has been at some point an alternative model, and every debate and schism in the scientific community has been over a disagreement over which model should be used. Which leads us to the other thing I wanted to tell you:

All models are wrong.
My model is wrong. Your model is wrong. The map is not the territory. No model can accurately represent or completely explain the thing it is representing, and if it could, it would be rather useless. If I had a mile-to-the-mile scaled map of Great Britain with every rock, fence, and blade of grass on it, no one would use it because it would be rather tedious to use. Even then it would be outdated by the time a day has passed and buildings have undergone further construction and rocks have been moved and blades of grass have been cut short in the constant attempt to make sure one’s house does not look bad in the eyes of his neighbor. But some models are useful. Valence theory correctly predicts what will happen when I mix iron and sulphur. Mendelian genetics can tell me the potential outcomes of a green and yellow pea hybrid. Most of the theories we use have significant application to some sort of technology, and many have more models based on the higher models that are used for deeper explanation.
If you cannot accept one or both of these facts, perhaps you had better stick to some clear cut black and white hot button topic waiting for you in your news or science feed. But if you have the sincerity to admit that even though my model is imperfect yours may be too, continue reading to learn about an alternate model, as proposed by

Authority of Scripture

Which begs the question, how does the Bible come to us? Wasn't it just written by several ancient philosophers, kings, and historians? Many of the books mention the person who wrote them, and if it was written by another philosopher, it holds no more authority than Aristotle or Plato.

The difference between the Bible and Plato is, that even with a person who wrote the words, the content originated from the Spirit of God. In a similar way to how a translator can write something for a language without using any of the same words or phrasing, the author can have his own writing in his own style and it still be the words of God. The Bible itself attests to the fact that it was not written merely by people, but “Men moved by [God's] Holy Spirit spoke from God.”

Unity of Scripture

Additionally, even though the Bible is composed of many separate books, it is a unified document with a single story. Even though the written across millennia and copied by hand for even longer, it displays remarkable consistency, and manuscripts dating to before the time of Christ are nearly identical to the modern ones. Its consistency with itself is especially confirming of its accuracy - modern writers working for large well paying corporations seem nearly incapable of producing consistent lore for a single cannon across a series, and often have to resort to writing off books as non-cannon or using other complicated explanations to make things seem less self contradictory. The Bible, on the other hand, has few apparent contradictions and even those are understood and solved to people who consider what many passages have to say on the matter.

When I refer to the Bible as a “unified document,” I mean that it has a single theme, that Christ, who created us in a perfect world, came down to redeem us from the curse of sin, so that we can live forever with him in a reperfected world. While this theme does not show itself wholly throughout the Bible, every part of the Bible discusses some part of it, as Christ showed some disciples in Luke 24:27. The Bible in every part shows this theme by emphasizing the problem of sin, showing the way Christ must come, and showing the reasons Christ did come and how he will come again.

Note that the Bible purpose is not primarily instruction and wisdom for life - the purpose of the Bible, as presented by Christ in John 5, is to bring the knowledge of eternal life to people. The Old Testament was leading them to this because the promises, types, and prophecies all point to Christ (Matthew 5), so everything that happened to Christ during his life was planned and known to God, as is clearly shown throughout the gospels and pointed out in acts 2.

The Old and New Testaments

When Adam and Eve first fail God in disobedience, God does not destroy mankind as a whole, but after judgement for their sin gives promises that He will one day set them free from their curse. This cycle of sin and judgement and promise is repeated throughout the old testament by God's chosen people, and is clearly seen done by Abraham, Issac, Jacob, and the patriarchs, As God gave them each promises when they sinned. Further displayed in the old testament is God's character laid out - his faithful love to his people, his justice with mercy toward failing people, and his great and overarching compassion. All this is contrasted with his people - even at the best of times they fail to follow him, fail to fear him, fail to remember his commands and character. When you come to the end of the old testament, you see God has preserved and promised great things to his people, but there seems no possibility that things will ever get better.

The New Testament comes along and introduces us to Christ, who meets every need the human race has. It shows his perfect leadership as a king and perfect priesthood as he asks God on our behalf, and he is the perfect prophet whom, although most reject him, he successfully persuades a band of desciples to follow him with faith. When finally it comes to the end of each gospel, it shows how Christ's great power to forgive sin was bought as he gives himself up as an offering to satisfy God's wrath accumulated for every sin of everyone in all of time. Finally, after the gospels, the rest of the letters and such expound God's plan going forward, explain each process in detail, and encourage us as fallen individuals in a local church to live harmoniously together for God.

Biblical Covenants

A third method of organizing the Bible's contents, and the method I personally consider the most valid, is by the five major covenants that God establishes with his people through the Bible. The first of these is the Noaic Covenant, an unconditional promise to all people and all animals that God will never again reset the entire earth, either with a flood or any other method of global destruction. He later makes the promise to Abraham that Abraham's offspring will own Canaan land, and that he will bless his offspring, and that through someone of his offspring the entire earth would receive his blessing. The covenant made with the children of Israel through Moses was designed to show the sins of the people and drive them to trust in God's grace, and it stood until being fulfilled and made obsolete by the New Covenant. As God makes more covenants, his promises grow larger and more specific, as we realize there will be some great offspring that will bring peace to the whole earth. This was focused by the Davidic covenant which promised that a great king of the dynasty of David would bring peace to Israel and the earth through an aeternal rule. Finally, through God's son, he makes a new covenant with anyone who trusts in him, that they will receive the blessing of aeternal life.

Application of Worldview

Perhaps one of the hardest parts about communicating a worldview is applying it, and among the easiest (and in my opinion, most unhelpful) worldviews to apply is the aforementioned CFRR worldview. As an example of application, I will be analyzing a certain short form video content found on YouTube, the sciency kind that I often watch. In this video, How Science Finally Made You Like Brussels Sprouts, Sam from Half as Interesting explains how genetic engineering has made brussels sprouts no longer very bitter, and consequently a preferred side dish in modern meals, while before it was generally only eaten by people with bad taste or exceptional cooking. Within this video, you can see how God created the wild mustard perfectly before it was ruined by the fall, and after centuries of artificial selection and genetic modification we can see the work of people fulfilling the creation mandate and using the plant life to create a likable meal from a formerly comparably awful plant. Some day in the future, it will be a perfect plant, and everyone will recognize God's creative work in building it, and his perfection in the design behind every gene. As can be seen, it is completely possible to use CFRR as a framework in which to interpret any viewpoint you see, however, it is not particularly useful by itself. Much more could have been said with a biblical worldview that specifically applies the attributes of God in creation or image of God (or lack thereof) in genetic modification, but CFRR is a very broad brush to use to interpret anything.
